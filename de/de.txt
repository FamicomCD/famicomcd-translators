strid "smbcd_info_alt_reality_desc"
string "The year is 1987. The NES/Famicom is seeing immense popularity hot off the success of the console and many of its titles during this period. Nintendo had released Super Mario Bros. 2 (known as <q>The Lost Levels</q> in the west) and published many popular action games the previous year.<br><br>Concurrently, the Super Famicom was in development and announced that year by the President of Nintendo. However, Nintendo wished to still support the original, now aging Famicom. This would become the FamicomCD, a CD-ROM based add-on that would be able to utilize CDs to load video games, presented as a cheaper entry point for existing Famicom users into the <q>next generation</q>. This would be a very ambitious endeavor, so to sell the system, it was requested that a technologically improved Super Mario Bros. must be able to be played on this device.<br><br>However, as Hiroshi Yamauchi said in September 1987, <q>Super Mario Bros. 4</q> and many other titles were already in the planning stages for the next version of the Famicom, which left <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nintendo_Research_%26_Development_1">Nintendo Research & Development Group #1</a> to be the ones to develop the Super Mario Bros. game for FamicomCD rather than the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nintendo_Research_%26_Development_4">Research Group #4</a> (who had worked on a lot of the previous entries and eventually "Super Mario World"), with assistance from Team Shikamaru for much of the planning and some parts of development. The game would look familiar to Super Mario Bros. players, but feature vibrant, colorful, and stunning visuals that weren't possible on the original Famicom, and sounds that were unlike anything from before.<br><br>It was initially planned to make the music of the game match the sound aesthetic of the Famicom games but with higher fidelity instruments, however the new CD spec meant that music was no longer limited by the sound polyphony and synthesis abilities of the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricoh_2A03">Famicom's 2A03 chip</a>. There were virtually no limitations in place, sounds could be as rich and full as they could ever sound. This led the production of the music for Super Mario Bros. CD to spin off into being a much more ambitious project, with more musicians and instrumentalists involved to truly make the music take advantage of the new technology."
